About

Jath Taluka , located in the easternmost part of Sangli district in Maharashtra, is the largest taluka in the district by area, covering approximately 2,240 square kilometers. The taluka headquarters, Jath town, serves as a vital administrative and commercial center, well connected by roads and near the Jat Road railway station. Jath is known for its dry and semi-arid climate, receiving limited rainfall annually, which significantly influences the region's agriculture. Despite these challenges, the local economy thrives on rainfed and well-irrigated farming, with major crops including groundnut, bajra, jowar, cotton, and gram. The region is also known for its vibrant weekly markets and a bustling cattle trade that attracts people from surrounding areas.

Historically, Jath holds great significance as a former princely state ruled by the Dafale Maratha dynasty before joining the Indian Union in 1948. Remnants of this heritage, including the ruins of old forts and royal structures, still stand today. The area is rich in culture and spirituality, hosting several historic temples such as the Yallamma, Rankesvar, and Ambabai temples, which draw large crowds during festivals and fairs. The Yallamma fair in particular is one of the most celebrated events, drawing tens of thousands of devotees. The population of Jath Taluka is predominantly rural, with a strong sense of community rooted in tradition, language, and local festivals. With a population of over 3.2 lakh people as per the 2011 Census, Jath continues to grow while preserving its distinct cultural and historical identity.

Urad Dal

Urad dal is a short-duration pulse crop grown mainly in India during kharif, rabi, and summer seasons. It grows well in warm, humid climates and loamy to black soils. The crop matures in 70‚Äì90 days and enriches the soil by fixing nitrogen. Basic fertilizers, seed treatment, and weed control are important for good yield. It requires 2‚Äì3 irrigations in summer. Common issues include yellow mosaic virus and pod borers. Harvesting is done when pods turn black. Average yield is 6‚Äì15 quintals per hectare depending on conditions.

Millets grow well in sandy to loamy soils and warm climates, with a growing period of 70‚Äì120 days depending on the variety. They are usually sown during the kharif season (June‚ÄìJuly), but some are also grown in rabi or summer. Seed treatment and timely weeding are essential. Maize grows well in well-drained loamy soil and requires good sunlight and moderate rainfall.
